What Nobody Tells You About
Buying Your First Home in LA

March 2026 5 min read By Laura D'Hernandez

After 17 years helping families buy and sell in the Los Angeles market, I can tell you that there are things most buyers only learn the hard way. This article is my attempt to change that — especially for first-time buyers who deserve straight answers, not real estate jargon.

1. Pre-approval is not the same as pre-qualification

This is probably the most important thing I tell every new buyer. Pre-qualification is a casual estimate. Pre-approval means a lender has actually reviewed your financial documents and committed to a loan amount. In a competitive market like LA, sellers won't take you seriously without a real pre-approval letter.

Laura's Tip

Get pre-approved before you start looking at homes — not after you fall in love with one. The process takes 1–3 days and removes a huge amount of stress.

2. Down Payment Assistance is real — and most buyers don't know about it

California has multiple programs that can help first-time buyers with their down payment. These programs change frequently, and most buyers — and even some agents — don't know they exist or how to access them. I specialize in finding these programs for my clients.

Some programs cover 3–5% of the purchase price as a deferred loan. Others are grants that don't need to be paid back. The eligibility requirements vary, but many of my clients have qualified.

"I thought homeownership was years away. Laura found a program that covered most of our down payment. We closed in 60 days." — A recent client

3. The 28/36 rule will save you from overbuying

Most people set their budget based on how much they want to spend — not how much they can comfortably afford. The 28/36 rule is the framework lenders use:

  • 28% — Total housing costs (mortgage + taxes + insurance) should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income.
  • 36% — Total monthly debt (housing + car + credit cards) should not exceed 36% of gross income.

4. The neighborhood matters more than the house

I've seen buyers fall in love with a beautiful home in a neighborhood they've never visited at night, on a weekend, or during rush hour. The house can be renovated. The location cannot be moved.

Before any offer, visit the neighborhood at different times of day, check commute times during actual rush hours, and look into local schools even if you don't have children (it affects resale value significantly).

5. Your agent works for you — choose carefully

As a buyer, you typically don't pay your agent directly. The seller pays the commission that is then split. This means your agent's service costs you nothing — so there's no reason to accept mediocre representation.
